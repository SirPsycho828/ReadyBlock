Section 01

Logo System

The ReadyBlock mark is a neighborhood of connected homes — a captain's house at the top, neighbors on either side, and your home as an amber map pin at the center. It says everything without a word.

Primary mark — light, dark, and color backgrounds

On light / paper
On brand teal
On dark / storm mode

Logo anatomy

The mark has four intentional elements, each carrying meaning:

Captain's house Dark teal at top — the community leader, the hub, the organizing force.
Neighbor houses Light teal flanking — your community, present and connected.
Map pin — your home Amber pin at center — you are located, you are findable, you belong here.
Connection lines Muted strokes linking all homes — the invisible network that makes a block.
Minimum size: 32×32px
Clear space: ½ × pin height on all sides
Wordmark: DM Serif Display, tight tracking
Never separate mark from wordmark
at sizes below 120px wide

Section 04

Voice & Tone

ReadyBlock sounds like the neighbor who has their act together — warm, competent, already thinking ahead. Not a government alert system. Not a Silicon Valley app. A trusted person on your street.

Brand voice principles

01
Neighborly, not corporate
Every notification, label, and error message should sound like it came from someone you trust — not a government agency or a tech startup. Before publishing any copy, ask: "Could my most trusted neighbor have said this?"
02
Calm, not alarming
ReadyBlock lives on people's phones 364 days a year without a disaster. The everyday register must be warm and confident. Reserve urgency for Storm Mode — use it sparingly so it lands with full weight when it matters.
03
Collective, not individual
The product is about community. Language should reflect that. "Your block" over "your profile." "Together" over "you." When someone does something good, the block benefits — make that visible in how you write about it.

Do this / not that

✓ Sound like ReadyBlock
"Your block just got stronger."
"6 of 12 households on your block are ready."
"Your neighbors are counting on you."
"When it matters most, you'll know who to call."
"Time to check in on your block."
"Your block knows you're safe."
"One tap. Family notified."
✗ Doesn't sound like ReadyBlock
"Optimize your disaster preparedness score."
"Emergency alert: complete your profile."
"You have not registered your resources."
"Leverage community assets in crisis scenarios."
"Sign up to prepare for the next disaster."
"Action required: update your emergency contacts."
"Vulnerability assessment incomplete."

Mode transition rules

Blue Sky → Storm Mode
  • Background shifts from Hearthstone to Storm Deep
  • All surfaces darken by 2 stops on the teal ramp
  • Amber accent activates for the STORM MODE ACTIVE banner
  • Primary CTA color shifts from Block Teal to Signal (#4DB8A0)
  • Resource locations become visible on the map
  • Typography gains 0.02em tracking for legibility under stress
Never do in Storm Mode
  • Don't use pure red — it triggers alarm response, not action
  • Don't animate decoratively — every motion should communicate
  • Don't reduce type size — legibility under stress is non-negotiable
  • Don't add new navigation — reduce choices, not add them
  • Don't use playful or casual copy — tone shifts to clear and direct
  • Don't show ads, promos, or upgrade nudges
Section 06

UI Components

Every component exists in two states: Blue Sky and Storm. Build components once, style them for both contexts using the mode token system.

Buttons

Primary & Secondary
Blue Sky mode. Primary: Block Teal fill. Secondary: teal outline, transparent fill.
Storm Mode CTAs
Storm Mode only. Signal teal for "I'm OK," coral for SOS. Never swap these.
Accent & Ghost
Amber for high-value actions that build block readiness. Ghost for secondary/dismiss.

Section 07

Imagery & Icons

ReadyBlock's visual language is warm, specific, and deeply local. Real Asheville streets. Real neighborhoods. People who look like they actually live here.

Photography direction

Front porches, real streets
Neighborhood texture
Real WNC homes, Asheville streets, local landmarks. Specificity builds trust.
Neighbors together
People in community
Groups of 2–4 people. Candid, not posed. Warm light. Eye contact encouraged.
Recovery, not just disaster
Community in action
Show recovery and mutual aid — not destruction. Hope, not fear.

What to avoid in imagery

🚨
No disaster porn
Flood images, destruction, collapsed buildings
🎖️
No military/tactical aesthetic
Camo, emergency vests, helmets, command centers
📱
No staged stock photos
Generic smiling families, generic city skylines
🔴
No red-dominant imagery
Creates anxiety in everyday app context
Section 08

Brand Don'ts

The mark is a neighborhood. Treat it like one — with care and respect. These are the things that break the brand.

✗ Never stretch or distort the mark

The house proportions are fixed. Never scale x and y independently.

✗ Never use on a busy background

The mark needs breathing room. Solid or very simple backgrounds only.

✗ Never recolor the amber pin

The amber map pin is the brand's most specific element. It does not come in other colors.

✗ Never replace the wordmark font

DM Serif Display is the only approved typeface for the "ReadyBlock" wordmark.

✗ Never add drop shadows or effects

The mark is flat by design. No gradients, glows, or dimensional effects.

✗ Never use red as a primary brand color

Coral (#E05C3A) is the only red-adjacent color allowed, and only for the SOS / Need Help button in Storm Mode.

The test

Before any design goes out: "Does this look like it was made by a neighbor who cares, or by a brand team that doesn't?" If you're not sure, it's the second one. Fix it.
